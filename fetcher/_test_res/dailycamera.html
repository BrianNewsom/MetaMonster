Lawsuit against Boulder seeks to spur development in northern 'planning reserve'

Couple asks court to force annexation of north Boulder properties

By Erica Meltzer

Staff Writer

Posted:   02/18/2016 04:45:13 PM MST
Updated:   02/18/2016 09:03:59 PM MST

Bo and Meredith Parfet are suing the city of Boulder and Boulder County in a bid to force the annexation of part of the 80 acres of land in unincorporated county that they own along U.S. 36 in north Boulder. The Parfets would like to build a housing development in that area, but first it must be annexed. (Cliff Grassmick / Staff Photographer)

A couple seeking to develop more than 80 acres of land north of Boulder sued the city and county this week in an effort to force the annexation of a portion of their property, which could undo restrictions that have limited the growth of Boulder for decades.

Bo and Meredith Parfet bought five parcels of land in unincorporated Boulder County, on the north side of U.S. 36, in 2014 for a combined $6.5 million with the idea of building a Holiday-style mixed-income neighborhood.

They call the project SamaBoulder, after a Sanskrit word that Meredith Parfet said means balance and that is also rendered as tranquility or equality.

The Parfets knew the land was in what's called Area III-Planning Reserve, meaning it is not currently eligible for annexation into Boulder under Boulder Valley Comprehensive Plan policies agreed to by the city and the county.

Under the county's rural residential zoning, they could build just a few houses on the properties. Annexation into the city is the key to fulfilling the Parfets' development vision.

The couple had been hoping the City Council would consider allowing development in Area III-Planning Reserve as part of this year's update to the Boulder Valley Comprehensive Plan.

"We believe that now is the time to bring this forward," Meredith Parfet said Thursday. "We've delayed too long to create mixed-income communities where a diverse range of people can live."

The Planning Reserve is roughly 500 acres north of Jay Road and east of U.S. 36 that could be annexed into the city with the mutual agreement of the Boulder Planning Board, the Boulder City Council, the Boulder County Planning Commission and the Board of County Commissioners for projects that had "sufficient merit" and meet "unmet need."

But last August, the City Council turned down the pleas of developers and advocates of workforce housing who said the lack of new housing in Boulder had reached a crisis point and that development of the Planning Reserve should be considered.

Council members said Boulder should develop land within the city limits first and not take on a contentious land-use change in the midst of heightened passions around growth and development.

The Parfets asked for an annexation feasibility study and were turned down. The city and the county also declined to take up a request for a service-area study to see whether the properties should be moved into Area II, which is eligible for annexation and considered appropriate for development.

Taking annexation issue to court

Now the Parfets are asking a Boulder District Court judge for a declaratory judgment to determine whether two of the parcels — 7.16 acres at 4756 28th St. and 14.28 acres at 0 Yarmouth Ave., which are both bordered by the city proper on the south — constitute an enclave of the city, and whether Boulder is therefore obligated to annex them.

The annexation of the two parcels that are contiguous to the city would extend the city limits, which would then potentially make the other three parcels eligible for annexation as well.

Boulder spokesman Ben Irwin confirmed the city had received the complaint Thursday, but said he could not comment on ongoing litigation.

However, Irwin said the definition of an enclave is unincorporated areas of land entirely contained within the outer boundary of the city. There are several enclaves in north Boulder and Gunbarrel.

In this case, attorney Rich Lopez is arguing that the properties owned by the Parfets' Yarmouth Holdings are effectively an enclave because the city owns as open space nearly all the land to the north and east of the five parcels, and the city's borders are to the south and west.

Lopez acknowledged the argument is novel, and that there is no precedent in Colorado law for defining an enclave that way. However, he thinks he has a sound argument. The properties are contiguous with the city, within its planning service area and not eligible to be annexed to any other jurisdiction.

"If it walks like a duck ... ," he said. "And when you're an enclave, annexation is not discretionary. We want a judge to determine if this is an enclave."

Lopez said the purpose of asking for clarification isn't just to get his clients' 20 acres into the city. It's to open up the question of developing the Planning Reserve. Lopez, a former City Council member, said the Planning Reserve was never intended to be open space forever, and those who created it thought it would be considered for development by now.

"I would hope that if we're successful, the city will say, 'If you slow down, let's do some subcommunity planning for Area III,'" Lopez said. "It creates an opportunity. We want to work with the city."

Asked if the complaint would undo the "four-body review" planning regime that has governed Boulder's northern urban boundary, one in which it only takes one entity saying no to doom a proposal, Lopez said, "Yes."

'We have redevelopment opportunities'

City Councilwoman Lisa Morzel, who has been a strong advocate for not developing the Planning Reserve and maintaining the four-body review, called the lawsuit "unfortunate."

She said it's good planning to make use of land within the city first before looking outside of it.

"My issue has always been that we have parking lots, we have redevelopment opportunities, we have sites in Area II that are eligible for annexation," Morzel said.

When the time comes to look again at the Planning Reserve, that's something that should be a policy decision by the relevant bodies.

"These guys can have their day in court, and we will, I'm sure, be advised by our legal staff, but our land-use policies have stood the test of time and challenges," she said. "I believe we are within our purview to have denied this."

City Councilman Andrew Shoemaker said he had no comment on the lawsuit, but he does believe Area III-Planning Reserve deserves another look, particularly with the intergovernmental agreement with the county that requires four-body review expiring in 2017.

Regardless of the outcome of the Parfets' complaint, that expiration and the question of whether it should be renewed are likely to revive the discussion next year.

"Market forces have hollowed out the middle in Boulder," Shoemaker said. "We need to be having a discussion about a variety of options, and that includes the Planning Reserve. The Planning Reserve can be part of the solution."

Meredith Parfet said setting aside the Planning Reserve may have been a good decision in the past, but the current system has produced "gridlock."

"Our belief is that this process has had many positive elements for the community, but it's become increasingly politicized," she said.

Parfet said the Planning Reserve is the most feasible place to build the affordable and middle-income housing Boulder says it wants because land prices are a tenth of what they are in the city.

Of course, land prices are so low there because an owner can't put much housing on them. Parfet acknowledged that fact, but noted that she and her husband could put up a few "McMansions" on that land and still turn a profit. They would rather do something that benefits the entire community, she said.

"We believe this land has the opportunity to be a really diverse, mixed community and really serve some of the people of Boulder," she said. "This is a mission for us."
